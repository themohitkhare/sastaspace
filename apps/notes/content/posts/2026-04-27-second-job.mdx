---
title: How to keep a side project from becoming a second job.
date: 2026-04-27
summary: The trick is making the project cheap enough to neglect and useful enough to revisit. Most projects fail at one or the other.
---

Most side projects die one of two ways. Either they get too big — a real user shows up, the codebase grows, and suddenly you owe a stranger uptime you never agreed to. Or they get too small — a single MDX file that you forget about until you stumble into it during a future grep.

The middle path is harder than it looks.

The version that worked for me, after a lot of failed ones:

**Make the cost of leaving it alone close to zero.** No paid services that bill while you sleep. No certificates that expire. No databases that need migrations to keep working. The lab here runs on one box and one tunnel. If I disappear for a year, it keeps serving the last build until something on Cloudflare's side changes.

**Write what the project does in a sentence in a place you'll see again.** For me that's a `summary:` line in the post's frontmatter. Six months from now I'm going to skim the index and that one sentence has to do all the work of reminding me what I was thinking. If I can't write it in a sentence, the project isn't done thinking yet.

**Give yourself permission to ship half-built.** A status chip that says `wip` or `paused` is a feature, not a confession. The brand here lets a project sit in `wip` forever. The thing that kills side projects is the imaginary expectation that the next push has to make it production-ready. The real expectation is: the next push has to make it slightly less broken.

**Don't add users you don't want.** If you put a sign-up form on a thing, someone will sign up, and now you owe them. The default state of every project here is "open to read, hard to write" — comments require an email magic-link, the admin queue is gated to one identity. Friction is a feature when the alternative is a maintenance burden.

The math I keep coming back to: a side project should owe you, not the other way around. If you're working on it because you want to, it's a workshop. If you're working on it because you have to, it's already a job. The brand on this page exists in part to remind me which side of that line I'm on.
